## Overall Vibe (what the user feels immediately)

You land on the page and it feels like:

> **A detective’s case file spread out on a wooden table.**
> Old paper. Notes. A map. A photo taped down. Clues everywhere.

Nothing is flashy. Nothing is moving.
It’s quiet, intentional, and *invites exploration*.

---

## Full-Page Composition

The entire screen is taken up by **one large sheet of paper** lying flat on a desk.

* Slightly yellowed paper
* Soft shadows around the edges
* Looks *handled*, not pristine

This paper is the **map** — the central artifact.

---

## Centerpiece: The Map

### What the map is

Not a real geographic map — more like:

* A textured, aged parchment
* Faint lines, grid, or abstract contours
* Think “planning map”, not “Google Maps”

It fills most of the left side.

---

### The taped photo (top-left area of the map)

* A photo of **you**, slightly rotated
* Two pieces of yellowed tape holding it down
* The tape overlaps the photo edges imperfectly

Under the photo, handwritten or typed:

> *“Field notes — Munich lab”*
> (or wherever you prefer)

This instantly answers:

> “Whose desk is this?”

---

## Interactive Hotspots (very important)

Scattered across the map are **clearly clickable items**.

They are NOT buttons in the traditional sense.

They appear as:

* Soft white rectangular outlines
* Slightly translucent
* Minimal icons inside (lines, books, screens)

When you hover:

* Border becomes solid white
* Object lifts slightly

These hotspots represent:

* **Projects** (file drawer icon)
* **CAD** (screen / console icon)
* **OpenFOAM** (book spines icon)

The user doesn’t need text yet — curiosity does the work.

---

## Bottom Area: Sticky Notes

Along the bottom edge of the map:

### Sticky notes casually placed

* Yellow paper
* Handwritten font
* Slight rotations

Each note has **high-level clues**:

* “Aerospace · CFD”
* “OpenFOAM · Python · ParaView”
* “Visualization tools”
* “Contact info”

They’re not primary navigation — they’re **context**.

This feels like:

> The investigator jotting down key facts.

---

## Right-Hand Panel: Case Summary

On the right side is a **slim info panel**, darker and calmer.

It contains:

### Header

> **Detective file — Chinmay**

Short paragraph:

* Who you are
* What kind of engineer you are
* One sentence max

Example tone:

> “CFD engineer building simulation pipelines, visual tools, and interactive models.”

This panel reassures users who want **clarity before exploration**.

---

## Bottom-Right: “Look Around” Button

A single, confident button:

> **Look around**

* Rounded
* White
* Simple

This is the *only explicit call to action*.

Mentally, it says:

> “Alright, you’ve seen the board — now explore the room.”

Clicking it takes the user to the **hub environment** where lockers, shelves, drawers, etc. live.

---

## Interaction Philosophy (crucial)

There are:

* ❌ No page transitions
* ❌ No forced order
* ❌ No animation overload

Instead:

* Everything clickable is clearly outlined
* The user decides what to inspect
* Feels like a point-and-click game from the 90s

This aligns *perfectly* with your “old click and go game” idea.

---

## What the LandingMap is *not*

It is NOT:

* A hero section
* A scrolling page
* A list of links
* A gimmick animation

It’s an **establishing shot**.

This primes them emotionally before content.
